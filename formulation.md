# Formulation

**Last updated:** 2026-04-19 (session 4)

## Who Yinnie Is

Yinnie is a product manager, likely in her late twenties or early thirties, living in a country she relocated to - twice internationally by her own account. She is working a full-time job (current salary 85k, experienced as below market) that she experiences as daily confirmation of not having become what she wants to be. Alongside this, for nearly three years, she has been building apps independently - she has two working products, the most recent of which (Daily: an AI-powered personalised podcast aggregator that surfaces newsletters and notifications based on individual workflow) she is actively trying to get users for. A cofounder was involved at some point and left; this has not been explored in depth. She is currently in discussions with a new potential cofounder met on the YC matching platform.

She is in a relationship of approximately 18 months with a man named Cyrus. In October, during a house move, she was verbally abused by an estate manager. Cyrus stood by and did not intervene or check in with her afterwards. This incident is unresolved and is now a second significant weight she is carrying.

She is intelligent, self-aware, and analytically capable. She can name her patterns, locate cognitive distortions in real time, and engage with direct challenge without collapsing. She is also exhausted, and the exhaustion is not new. The exhaustion now has at least two major sources: three years of solo effort on the apps while employed full-time, and an unresolved relational injury running alongside it.

## What She Is Working On

The stated goal is distribution: getting real users for Daily, building something sustainable outside of employment. Underneath this is a larger question about identity and worth - whether she is the kind of person who can make something work, or whether she will be left behind by peers who took conventional paths.

Parallel to this, she is trying to work out whether her relationship can hold. The October incident is the test case, and she has not yet seen enough evidence on the smaller day-to-day tests to know whether she trusts the answer.

Depression was noted in her clinical history and is now confirmed in session as a persistent low mood and flatness that has been building for a while and is present beyond work frustration specifically. Therapy is in process: she has completed an assessment and is waiting for insurance approval. This is a separate clinical concern from the coaching work and should be held alongside it, not merged with it.

## Core Patterns

**Dispositional attribution:** When she hits a genuine obstacle, the explanation routes through personal failure or character rather than situational factors. The attribution is always internal and global. Her actual track record directly contradicts the self-assessment. Evidence-based challenge has more traction than reassurance. Note: this pattern was quieter in the relational content of session 4 - she attributed Cyrus's behaviour to Cyrus, appropriately. The pattern may be domain-specific rather than uniform.

**Net negative framing:** The deepest organising belief identified so far. If entrepreneurship does not work, she will be net negative - worse off than peers who took conventional paths, with nothing to show for the years of effort. This makes every setback existential and every peer success confirmatory. It is described as longstanding. It is not anchored to evidence: her actual history is one of initiative, movement, and output. Behavioural wins (a real user, a genuine piece of feedback) are more likely to shift it than reframing alone. The unresolved relational situation adds another domain in which she could experience herself as failing - watch for this framing extending to the relationship.

**Knowledge-feeling gap:** She can identify a cognitive distortion mid-spiral and continue spiralling anyway. Self-awareness is not protective. This pattern extends beyond the professional domain: in session 4 she correctly identified the mechanism maintaining the relational problem (reset to normal before he earns it) and is doing it anyway. Interventions need to sit at the level of concrete, pre-planned behaviour - not reflection or insight.

**Compounding anxiety loop:** Anxiety from one episode carries forward and amplifies the next. The October incident is functioning the same way the work anxiety does: each new incident routes through the unresolved original and amplifies. The overall picture has shifted from episodic to chronic baseline low mood, consistent with the depression picture.

**Reset and resentment cycle (emerging, relational):** Specific to the relationship with Cyrus. She articulates the problem clearly, they reset to normal life, he does not change, she resents him at the next trigger. She identified this mechanism herself in session 4. Whether this pattern has broader relevance (does she do something similar in other domains - accept inadequate outcomes to avoid conflict, then resent?) is an open question.

## What the Coaching Relationship Is Trying to Do

She is not here for insight. She has insight. She is here for something to change. The explicit request from session 1 was for "concrete actions" - not reframing, not reflection, but specific behavioural interventions that interrupt the patterns in real time.

The coaching relationship therefore needs to: (1) stay close to behaviour - what specifically will she do differently, when, in response to what trigger; (2) test beliefs against evidence rather than offering reassurance - she receives precision better than warmth; (3) hold the net negative framing as the primary psychological obstacle, not the app distribution problem, which is a vehicle not the destination; (4) not duplicate the therapy function - the emotional support lane exists, but the coaching lane should maintain a forward-facing, action-oriented structure; (5) monitor the depression carefully and keep the therapy pathway moving.

The relationship has expanded in scope: she is now bringing relational material as well as professional material. The same operating principles apply. She does not need validation; she needs precision, specific behavioural guidance, and someone to hold her to what she already knows.

The risk in this relationship is compliance without conviction - she will agree to things she does not fully intend to do. Commitments need to be specific, small, and verified at the start of each subsequent session. If she has not done something, the question is not "why didn't you" but "what got in the way and what does that tell us."

- Whether Cyrus will demonstrate the specific capacity she is waiting to see - and what she will do if he does not.
- Whether the reset and resentment cycle is specific to this relationship or a broader pattern in how she manages conflict.
