# Pattern: Net Negative Framing

**First observed:** 2026-04-16 (session 2)

## Description

Yinnie holds a belief that if her entrepreneurial effort does not succeed, she will be "net negative" - that the years of work without conventional career advancement, the lower salary, and the sacrifice of rest and time will leave her worse off than peers who took conventional paths. This is not an occasional frustration thought; it is described as a longstanding frame ("for as long as I can remember") that has sharpened over the past two and a half years of sustained effort.

## Examples from Sessions

- **2026-04-16:** "Unless I succeed in being an entrepreneur, I just end up being that person who didn't have a life because I've been working all the time trying to make it work, and it didn't end up working. And my career is not excelling. So I'm net negative."
- **2026-04-18:** Not stated explicitly, but structurally present in the draw toward the cofounder prospect. Pull toward having a building partner after an exhausting solo path reads in part as an exit from the net negative trajectory.
- **2026-04-21:** Direct activation: "I've been working so hard for the past 2 years for absolutely no results." When challenged, she sharpened the claim: "No results means no users, no point in having a product no one uses." New element: she pushed back on the reframe explicitly - "I don't want you to glaze this over."
- **2026-04-26:** Present in the pre-calculation of the Rachel follow-up failing ("she's probably gonna give me a lukewarm response") and in "no more fuel to give." However, she also made a genuinely grounded assessment of Daily that was NOT catastrophising: "No one is using the product. We can just admit that and move on." She distinguished between "Daily isn't working" and "I should give up on building," arriving at: "I want to build, but I don't know what to build. I'm exhausted." This is the first time the grounded assessment and the net negative narrative have been visibly separate in the same session.

## Clinical Notes

This frame is load-bearing. It means every day that passes without entrepreneurial success is experienced as a day that makes her final position worse. Every peer success is further evidence. Every slow data point from the app confirms the trajectory. It makes routine setbacks feel existential.

The frame is factually miscalibrated. Yinnie has relocated internationally twice, left a toxic role, shipped two working applications while employed full-time, and sustained a focused effort for nearly three years. These are not the actions or outputs of someone in a net negative position.

This pattern interacts with dispositional attribution: obstacles are not situational problems to solve, they are confirmations of the net negative trajectory. It also feeds the compounding anxiety loop: each setback does not discharge but accumulates as evidence of the prediction coming true.

**Session 6 update:** Simple counter-evidence no longer suffices. She is now actively resisting reframes she experiences as positive glossing.

**Session 7 update - potential inflection point:** For the first time, she made a grounded product assessment (Daily has no PMF, targeting is too generic) that was separate from the net negative narrative. She could hold "this product isn't working" without it becoming "I am failing at everything." The distinction was fragile - "I feel like giving up" and "I'm very defeated" were present in the same session - but the capacity to separate real data from global self-verdict is new. The coaching challenge: reinforce this separation. When she makes an accurate assessment, hold it as accurate. When the assessment slides into the net negative frame ("no more fuel to give"), name the slide.

Behavioural evidence (a cofounder who wants to work with her, a coffee that goes well, one person who uses something she builds) is more likely to move this belief than reframing.

## Watch For

Any time she frames a setback as confirming she is "behind" or that others are "ahead." The comparison to specific peers is this pattern in action. Name the frame, then go to the specific problem rather than the counter-argument. Watch for the distinction between grounded assessment and net negative spiral - reinforce the former, name the latter. Watch for "I don't want you to glaze this over" or similar language indicating the reframe is being experienced as dismissal. Watch for pre-calculation of failure before action ("she's probably not going to respond") as a variant of this pattern.
