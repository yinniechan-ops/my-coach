# Pattern: Dispositional Attribution

**First observed:** 2026-03-27 (session 1)

## Description

When Yinnie hits a real obstacle or feels blocked, she routes the explanation through personal failure - character, willingness, or identity - rather than situational factors or specific skill gaps. The attribution is internal and global, not external or specific.

Important qualification established across sessions 4-5: this pattern appears to be domain-specific rather than uniform. It is most active in ambiguous professional or entrepreneurial situations where external confirmation is absent. In interpersonal situations where the other person's behaviour is clearly attributable to the other person, the pattern is notably quieter.

## Examples from Sessions

- **2026-03-27:** Cannot get users for her apps - concludes she does not want it badly enough and is "the person stopping herself." Actual obstacle: one specific distribution method (public content creation) conflicts with her social anxiety and preference for privacy.
- **2026-03-27:** Will not post on LinkedIn - frames this as a character flaw rather than a specific behaviour that is hard for a specific reason.
- **2026-03-27:** Did not ask questions in a meeting - concludes she does not know what she is doing, rather than: she was anxious about how a specific question would land.
- **2026-04-16:** Low sign-up rate from 50 invites became "maybe it's just not the right thing" and "should I give up" - jumping from a specific data point to a global conclusion about the product and her judgment.
- **2026-04-18:** Ambiguity from a cofounder prospect routed immediately to "maybe it's not me" and "should I just take the hint." Actual data: the prospect had asked to be kept updated ten minutes prior.
- **2026-04-19 (session 4):** Pattern was quiet in the relational content. She attributed Cyrus's behaviour to Cyrus, appropriately.
- **2026-04-19 (session 5):** Cofounder closure handled without self-blame. She read one non-committal reply as a no, said "we move forward," and that was it. Same ambiguous-signal situation that triggered the pattern in session 3, but no dispositional attribution this time.

## Clinical Notes

She is partially aware of this pattern. The reframe that lands best is precision: pointing to her actual track record, or examining the specific obstacle rather than accepting the global attribution. She engages with evidence-based challenge more readily than reassurance.

The domain-specificity is now reasonably established. The pattern is most active when:
- The situation is professionally or entrepreneurially high-stakes
- Confirmation or positive signal from an external party is absent
- She has been in sustained effort without results

The pattern is quieter when:
- The other person's behaviour is clearly the relevant factor (relational contexts)
- The stakes are lower or she has less identity investment
- She has already done the work of sending a clear message and is assessing the response

This domain-specificity is clinically significant. It means the net negative framing - which is about the entrepreneurial domain specifically - is likely to be the primary driver of the pattern, rather than a generalised self-critical schema.

## Watch For

Any time she uses "I just can't" or "I'm not willing" language around a specific behaviour, or jumps from one data point to a verdict about herself or the product. Test whether she has actually tried alternatives or whether the conclusion is drawn from one blocked path. In interpersonal situations: watch for "taking the hint" when no clear hint exists - though this appears less active in interpersonal contexts now.
